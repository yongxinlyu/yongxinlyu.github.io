The AI Hype for Literature Review

January 06, 2025

2025   ·   research   AI  

AI is the rising star in research, not just as a tool within projects but also as a helper for tasks like writing papers and conducting literature reviews. If you search online for “AI tools for literature review”, you’ll be overwhelmed by tutorials, articles, and an endless list of tools claiming to revolutionize how we sift through academic papers. Yet, amidst all this noise, I’ve found little critical discussion about how these tools compare to traditional approaches–or how to use them effectively.

So, I thought, why not write the blog I wish I had found? Here it is–a candid take on AI tools for literature reviews, their strengths, limitations, and how we might combine them with human effort for the best results.

The Traditional Approach: Still Relevant?

The traditional way of conducting a literature review usually involves database searches (Google Scholar, Web of Science), following citation trails, keeping an eye on researchers’ profiles, and subscribing to keyword alerts or new publications by specific authors.

When I started my PhD, this process felt overwhelming. Back then, I was building foundational knowledge, and every paper felt like it could hold the key to understanding my field. Now, as a final-year PhD candidate, my literature searches are more targeted. I mostly look for cross-disciplinary insights or new developments in my niche area.

For the most part, I’ve found the traditional approach works well. It’s systematic and dependable. But there are those moments when I think, How did I miss this paper? or What if I’ve overlooked something critical? This is where the idea of speeding up the process with AI tools becomes tempting.

Enter AI Tools: What Do They Actually Do?

The AI tools I’ve explored fall into two main categories:

  1. Citation Network Visualization Tools (e.g., Connected Papers, Research Rabbit): These tools visualize relationships between papers, often based on citation data. Starting with a “seed” paper, they create a web of connected research, making it easier to see the academic links between authors and topics.

I find these tools helpful. They quantify what I’ve often tried to map out in my head–a visual network of who’s citing whom and where ideas intersect. For early-stage literature review, this can be a great way to explore the breadth of a field. (this is not new, can also be done without AI, how is AI done it differently?)

  1. Question-Answering Tools with Paper Summarization (e.g., Elicit): These promise to answer research questions by summarizing papers. The idea sounds great, but my experience with these tools has been disappointing. Here’s why:
    • When I tested Elicit on questions directly related to my niche research area–where I’ve spent years building expertise–it returned references that often irrelevent or low-quality (think papers in obscure, low-impact journals).
    • The summaries generated were fluffy, sometimes even inaccurate, likely because they were based on poor-quality references.

In short, these tools might work better in well-established research areas with lots of publications. But for niche or cutting-edge topics, they simply can’t replace a human expert’s ability to critically evaluate papers.

The Limitations of AI for Literature Revies

AI tools shine in areas where there’s an abndance of data and established patterns. But niche research–where the real excitement often lies–is a different story. These fields have fewer papers, making it harder for AI to distinguish high-quality research from noise. Without human judgement, the risk of relying on irrelevant or flawed references is high.

And then there’s the bigger issue: creativity (quote my supervisor). AI might be able to summarize, sort, or visualize data, but it doesn’t think critically or creatively. It can’t recognize a groundbreaking idea hiding in an unconventional paper–or see how two seemingly unrelated studies might combine to spark something new. That’s the realm of human expertise.

Combine AI and Human Effort: A Practical Approach

The key to use AI tools effectively is knowing when and how to combine them with traditional methods. Here’s what I’ve found works:

  • Early PhD stage: Use AI tools like Connected Papers to explore the breadth of your field and identify key papers quickly. At this stage, the goal is to get a broad overview, and AI can help save time.
  • Later PhD stage or experienced researchers: focus shifts to depth. Human effort becomes more critical for critically analyzing high-quality papers and uncovering nuanced insights. AI tools may still be helpful for exploratory searches in adjacent fields, but they should supplement–not replace–traditional methods.

Final Thoughts: Why Human Expertise Still Wins

At the end of the day, creativity and critical thinking remain the heart of impactful research. AI tools can assist by streamlining parts of the process, but they can’t replace the deep, thoughtful engagement required to truly understand and innovate.

If you’re a researcher, the best approach might be to treat AI tools as assistants–not experts. Use them to speed up repetitive tasks, but let you judgement guide the process. After all, the most groundbreaking ideas come from human curiosity and creativity, not algorithms.
