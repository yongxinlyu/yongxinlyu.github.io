Let’s be honest — doing a PhD doesn’t exactly come with a generous paycheck.

As an international PhD student at UNSW, I’ve navigated a range of financial challenges, especially after submitting my thesis. When the scholarship ends and full-time work isn’t yet an option due to student visa restrictions, what can you do? One answer: casual jobs on campus.

In this post, I want to share my experience and the opportunities I’ve discovered — opportunities I wish I had explored much earlier during my PhD journey. These roles are not only helpful financially, but they can also contribute to your personal growth, teaching skills, and even your future career prospects.

### Why I Initially Avoided Casual Jobs

During my PhD, I wasn’t too interested in taking on any side work. Living with my husband and sharing expenses meant that our scholarships just about covered our costs. I also wanted to dedicate all my time to research and assumed that casual roles wouldn’t add value to my academic career.

But once I submitted my thesis, that financial safety net disappeared — and I realized just how important casual work could be. It provided not just income, but structure, confidence, and connection during that uncertain post-submission period.

### 1. Teaching Opportunities (Highly Recommended!)

My top recommendation is to look into teaching-related roles. These include:

- **Lab demonstrations**
- **Tutoring**
- **Teaching assistant roles**
- **Marking assignments or exams**

What I didn’t fully appreciate during my PhD is that teaching experience is *hugely* valued in Australian academia. At UNSW and other institutions, teaching can be just as important as research when it comes to hiring or promotions.

I hesitated initially because:

- My school didn’t offer courses directly related to my research area.
- I didn’t see the value in engaging with undergraduate students.

But I was wrong on both counts.

You can look beyond your own school — even across faculties — to find relevant courses. Reach out to lecturers and express your interest. Even if there aren’t immediate openings, you’ll get on their radar for future terms.

**Tip:** Teaching roles are tied to the academic term, so start exploring at least one term in advance.

#### **Nervous About Teaching?**

I was too! I highly recommend training programs like UNSW’s [Beginning to Teach](https://www.teaching.unsw.edu.au/beginning-teach-ecrs-and-hdrs). It helped me build confidence, understand how to design and plan lessons, and learn what goes on behind the scenes. Teaching isn’t just about delivering a two-hour class — it’s about thoughtful preparation and structure that make the experience smooth and engaging.

### 2. Outreach Activities

Another fulfilling option is participating in outreach programs. These are often aimed at high school students, the general public, or underrepresented communities.

I volunteered with the [**UNSW STEMM Champions Program**](https://www.unsw.edu.au/science/engage-with-us/unsw-stemm-champions-program) and participated in events like:

- [**Pint of Science**](https://pintofscience.com.au) – where I gave a talk about my research in a pub!
- **Science in the Swamp** 
- **Women in Science blog** (here’s a [link to my post](https://blogs.unsw.edu.au/stemmchampions/blog/2023/06/)!)

Friends of mine have been involved in [**SciX**](https://www.unsw.edu.au/science/study-with-us/scix-school-extension-program), **Open Day**, and other public-facing events.

Some of these opportunities require significant time and effort, but they are incredibly rewarding. Many start as volunteer roles, but there are also **paid positions**, especially during school holidays or university open days.

These experiences help you:

- Meet people passionate about science communication
- Improve how you explain your research to different audiences
- Develop soft skills in public speaking, presentation, and storytelling

### 3. Exam Supervision

Exam supervision is another option I discovered recently (thanks to Mariana for introducing me!). These jobs typically take place at the end of each term and can span over 2–3 weeks.

I signed up partly for the income and partly out of curiosity — I had never taken an exam at UNSW myself and wanted to understand how large-scale assessments are managed. To my surprise, exams here are not supervised by teaching staff (like we did back in Hong Kong), but by a dedicated exams team — and most assessments are computer-based!

This is my first time this term. so At first, it was a bit overwhelming — I had to learn the rules while supervising. But it turned out to be a really interesting experience.

### **Final Thoughts**

If I could go back to the start of my PhD, I’d definitely get involved in these kinds of jobs earlier. Not just for the money — but for the **professional development, networking, and experience** they offer. You meet people from across disciplines, gain valuable soft skills, and better prepare for life after the PhD.

So if you’re an international PhD student (or any student!) at UNSW wondering how to stay afloat — or even just stay active while waiting on your thesis results — consider looking into casual roles. It might surprise you how much value they bring.
